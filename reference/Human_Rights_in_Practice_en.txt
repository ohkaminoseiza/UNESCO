Human Rights in Practice: What Human Rights Due Diligence Means Today

Human rights due diligence refers to a continuous process through which companies and organizations examine how their activities impact human rights, and take steps to prevent and address any harm that may arise. It is not merely a one-time "check," but a framework for responsible conduct that should be embedded in everyday business operations.

This concept gained global recognition with the adoption of the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs) in 2011 by the United Nations. These principles clarify that, alongside the state's duty to protect human rights, companies also bear a responsibility to respect them. Human rights due diligence is the practical method through which this responsibility is fulfilled.

In practice, the process generally follows several key steps. First, companies identify where risks to human rights may exist within their own operations and across their supply chains. Typical examples include labor conditions in overseas factories or the risk of child labor in sourcing raw materials. Next, they take measures to prevent or mitigate these risks. If harm does occur, they are expected to provide remedies to affected individuals. Finally, companies are required to communicate their efforts transparently to stakeholders.

A crucial point is that "not knowing" is no longer an acceptable excuse. In today's globalized economy, corporate activities extend far beyond a single organization, encompassing business partners and suppliers. As a result, human rights due diligence is understood not simply as legal compliance, but as a broader social responsibility.

Importantly, this approach is not limited to risk avoidance. When properly implemented, it can enhance corporate credibility and contribute to building a sustainable business foundation. In recent years, investors and consumers have increasingly paid attention to how companies address human rights, making due diligence a source of value creation rather than merely a cost.

Moreover, this framework is not solely a corporate issue. Each of us is also part of this structure—as consumers and as workers. The choices we make in our daily lives, such as which products and services we support, can indirectly influence human rights conditions around the world.

In essence, human rights due diligence is a system that encourages us to extend our imagination toward the rights of others who may not be immediately visible to us. In an era of globalization and digitalization, it asks us to continually reflect on how our actions affect the lives of people far beyond our immediate surroundings. This mindset represents a new form of responsibility required in contemporary society.
