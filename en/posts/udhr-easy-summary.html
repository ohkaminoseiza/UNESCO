A Plain-Language Summary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)

April 5, 2026 | Human Rights, Education

Human beings are born free, and each person possesses an inherent dignity and worth that cannot be replaced. This dignity is not something granted by others; it arises from the very fact of being human. For this reason, we are called to treat one another not as mere means, but as autonomous individuals, engaging with each other in a spirit of respect and compassion.

Differences such as race, gender, language, religion, origin, or social status do not determine a person's value. Rather, it is precisely within such diversity that the universal dignity of humanity becomes visible. The principle that all people possess equal rights is grounded in this universality.

Every person has the right to life and to live in safety. Any form of domination that denies one's humanity — such as slavery, torture, or cruel and degrading treatment — is a direct violation of human dignity and can never be justified under any circumstances.

All human beings must be treated equally under the law. State power must not be exercised arbitrarily, and every individual has the right to a fair and impartial process of justice. Here lies the core of modern legal thought: the limitation of power in order to protect human dignity.

Human beings are also endowed with inner freedom. The freedom to think, to believe, to question, and to express oneself is fundamental to living as a self-determining individual. The freedom to choose where to live, to belong to a community, and to seek protection in another country when facing persecution all extend from this autonomy.

At the same time, human beings exist within society. The right to work, to pursue one's livelihood under fair conditions, and to sustain a dignified life is essential. Education plays a crucial role in enabling individuals to grow as free and responsible persons, fostering an understanding of human dignity and freedom.

Participation in cultural and artistic life, as well as access to the benefits of scientific progress, are also fundamental to a fully human existence. Human beings do not merely survive; they create meaning and seek to understand the world.

These rights are not realized in isolation, but within relationships of mutual support in society. In this sense, every individual is not only a bearer of rights, but also a participant with responsibilities toward others.

Finally, these rights must never be arbitrarily restricted or taken away. States and institutions exist to protect human rights, not to undermine them. This principle stands as the foundation upon which all human rights must rest.
