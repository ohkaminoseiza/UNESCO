Why Do People Live in "Protected Nature"?

April 11, 2026 | Environment, Education

Understanding UNESCO Biosphere Reserves

When we hear the phrase "protecting nature," we often imagine excluding human activity as much as possible. However, this common assumption is challenged by the concept of Biosphere Reserves promoted by UNESCO.

A Biosphere Reserve is not simply a protected natural area. Rather, it is a place where people and nature coexist, and where new models of sustainable living are actively explored.

Biosphere Reserves are part of UNESCO's "Man and the Biosphere Programme." Their goals can be summarized into three key functions.

First, to conserve biodiversity and ecosystems. Second, to support sustainable economic and social development for local communities. And third, to promote research, education, and knowledge sharing.

What makes this approach unique is that it does not separate "protection" from "use." Instead of removing human presence, it seeks to reshape human activities so that they harmonize with nature.

This philosophy is reflected in the structure of Biosphere Reserves. Each reserve is organized into three zones. The "core area" is strictly protected, preserving natural ecosystems. The "buffer zone" allows activities such as research, education, and eco-tourism. The "transition area" is where people live and carry out agriculture, tourism, and other economic activities.

In other words, Biosphere Reserves create a system where protected nature and human society coexist within a single integrated framework.

This model has spread across the world. In Japan, places such as Yakushima, Hakusan, and the forest region of Yambaru are designated as Biosphere Reserves, each offering unique examples of how humans and nature can coexist.

Today, humanity faces global challenges such as climate change and biodiversity loss. These issues cannot be solved by a simple choice between environmental protection and economic growth.

Biosphere Reserves offer a different perspective. They demonstrate that transforming human activity itself into a sustainable form is the key to protecting nature.

In this sense, Biosphere Reserves are more than just a policy framework. They represent a way of rethinking how our societies function.

How should humans live within nature? And what kind of environment should we pass on to future generations? Biosphere Reserves offer one practical and forward-looking answer to these questions.
