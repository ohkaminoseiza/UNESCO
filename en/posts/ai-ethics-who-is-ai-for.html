Who Is AI For?
Reading UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence

April 5, 2026 | AI, Education

In recent years, artificial intelligence (AI) has rapidly made its way into our daily lives. AI is now used in a wide range of settings — smartphone apps, search engines, healthcare, finance, and education, to name just a few. Against this backdrop, in 2021 UNESCO adopted the Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence — the first international normative framework on AI agreed upon by 193 countries. What this recommendation calls into question is not merely a technical issue. It goes beyond asking "how should we use AI?" to ask something deeper: "what kind of society do we want to build?"

What AI's Progress Brings

AI has brought many benefits. In healthcare, it contributes to the early detection of disease; in the workplace, it improves efficiency. It also plays a role in expanding connections between people. At the same time, however, serious problems have come to light — the excessive collection of personal data and surveillance, discrimination driven by algorithms, and the widening of social inequality. These may appear to be new problems, but they are in fact characterized by AI's tendency to amplify existing inequalities. In light of this, UNESCO argues that AI should not be seen merely as a convenient technology, but as a force that affects all of human society.

The "Values" That Must Lie at the Heart of AI

What distinguishes this recommendation is its insistence that "values" must serve as the starting point for the design and use of AI. Human rights and dignity, social equity, and coexistence with the natural environment are held up as things that must be considered before efficiency or profit. In other words, the recommendation takes the position that AI should begin not from the question of "what can it do?" but from the question of "what is it permitted to do?" This reflects a strong determination not to embrace technology unconditionally, but to keep it under human control.

A Human Rights-Centered Approach to AI

UNESCO's recommendation holds that AI must be designed with human rights at its center. To this end, several key principles are required of AI systems. First, AI must not cause harm to people — safety is a prerequisite. Individual privacy must be rigorously protected. Furthermore, there must be a responsible party accountable for AI decisions; ambiguity about "who is responsible" is not acceptable. AI decision-making should be as transparent and understandable as possible, and it is vital that humans retain the authority to make final judgments. In addition, AI must function equitably within society and must be designed so as not to produce discrimination. These principles are not guidelines for engineers alone — they are social rules in which each and every one of us has a stake.

Concrete Problems That Cannot Be Ignored

One of the strengths of this recommendation is that it does not remain at the level of abstract principles, but identifies specific problems. For example, there is the issue of personal characteristics and preferences being inferred from social media and search histories, and that information being used for advertising or behavioral manipulation. In cases like AI-based loan assessments — where important decisions are made without human involvement — it becomes unclear who bears responsibility when an error occurs. Furthermore, when the data on which AI learns contains biases, those biases can manifest directly as discrimination. There are indeed reports from the medical field of differences in diagnostic accuracy based on skin color. These problems demonstrate that AI is not a "neutral technology." Rather, it is an entity that mirrors the structure of society — and sometimes amplifies it.

A Strong Message: "Prohibition"

There is one particularly noteworthy aspect of UNESCO's recommendation: it clearly specifies things that must not be done with AI. The most prominent example is the issue of social scoring and mass surveillance. Systems that quantify people's behavior and creditworthiness and then use those scores to determine how individuals are treated in society carry the risk of undermining human freedom and dignity. UNESCO therefore sounds a strong alarm about such uses. This represents a vitally important stance in the sense that it sets limits on technological progress.

AI Is a Challenge for Society as a Whole

The recommendation makes clear that AI is not only a matter for specialists and corporations — it is a theme that society as a whole must grapple with. AI touches every field: education, labor, the environment, gender, and culture. The ways in which AI development is changing employment, and the environmental costs of the enormous energy required to develop and run AI systems, are just two examples of issues that cannot be ignored. In short, AI is not simply an "IT issue" — it is a matter that bears on the very design of social institutions.

How to Move Forward in Practice

UNESCO also outlines concrete methods for putting this recommendation into practice. One is a mechanism through which countries can assess their own state of readiness. Another is the concept of an "ethical impact assessment" — a process for thinking through in advance the effects that AI may have on society. This involves asking questions such as "Who will be affected?" and "What kinds of harm might occur?" before a system is deployed. This stands in sharp contrast to the traditional approach of responding only after problems arise.

What This Recommendation Asks of Us

UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI is not merely a set of guidelines. It questions the very nature of what it means to be human in the age of AI. AI is indeed a powerful technology — but it is human beings who decide how it is used. And it is also each and every one of us who must participate in making those rules. The question facing society going forward is whether we will simply accept AI as "something convenient to use," or engage with it as subjects who actively think about how it ought to be used.

In Closing

What UNESCO's recommendation presents is a principle that is simple, yet weighty: "AI should exist for the benefit of humanity, and humanity must govern it." As AI continues to evolve, the question of how far we can uphold this principle is not a technological problem — it is a test of the maturity of our society.
